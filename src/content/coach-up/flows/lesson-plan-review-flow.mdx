# The Lesson-Plan Review Flow

*[The Session Design Framework](../frameworks/session-design-framework) builds the plan. This flow pressure-tests it before it meets a real court — so the weak spot gets caught on paper, where it's cheap to fix.*

---

## When it fires

In the days **before** you run a designed session — most often a [1:1](../systems/structure-a-1-1-lesson), [small group](../systems/run-a-small-group-lesson), or [clinic](../systems/design-a-clinic). Run it on yourself for every session; run it *with Sam* in [the weekly 1:1](weekly-1-1-flow) for the ones you're least sure of.

## The flow

Walk your [index-card session plan](../frameworks/session-design-framework) through six questions, in order:

### 1. Is the focus actually one thing?

Read your FOCUS line. If it's "third shots and dinks and footwork," it's three sessions pretending to be one. One block of [the Adult Skill Progression](../frameworks/the-adult-skill-progression). Cut until it's one.

### 2. Does the plan fit *these* players?

Read your PLAYERS line against your blocks. Right level? Does it serve each person's [running focus](../vocabulary/term-running-focus)? Built for the level spread you'll actually get? A plan that fits a generic player fits nobody.

### 3. Can you say what each block reps — in one sentence?

For every game and constraint: the one-sentence purpose. If you can't say it, the players can't feel it, and it doesn't belong in the session. This is the [Constraint Design Toolkit](../frameworks/the-constraint-design-toolkit)'s rule, applied as a checkpoint.

### 4. Does it climb?

Read the blocks in order. Does each one step up from the last — engineered success first, chaos added gradually, cooperative before competitive? If block one is the hardest thing in the session, the sequence is upside down.

### 5. Is the frame scripted, and does it sell the why?

Read your FRAME line. Is it actually written? Is it tight? Does it tell an adult *why this focus matters to them*? A vague or improvised frame loses the room in the first two minutes.

### 6. Do you have an off-ramp for every block?

Read your OFF-RAMPS line. For each game, your loosening move if the room collapses. Decided now, calm — not later, scrambling. A block with no off-ramp is a block you can't rescue.

## What a "fail" means

A question that doesn't pass isn't a disaster — it's the flow *working*. You caught it on paper. Fix the plan and re-run the question. The whole point is that a weak design is cheap to fix here and expensive to fix in front of paying adults.

## With Sam

In [the 1:1](weekly-1-1-flow), Sam runs these same six questions on your plan — but the move is *questions, not answers*. Sam asks "what does this block rep?" and lets you find the gap, because catching your own weak design is the skill you're actually building. The flow is the same whether you run it solo or with a mentor; the mentor just makes you sharper at running it on yourself.

> Six questions before the court: one focus, fits these players, every block has a one-sentence purpose, it climbs, the frame is scripted, every block has an off-ramp. Catch it on paper.
