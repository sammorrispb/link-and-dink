# The First-Event Flow

*Coach Up puts you in front of real players in week two — "real reps from week one" is the promise. This is the runbook for that first solo event, so the nerves have a structure to stand on.*

---

## When it fires

Around **week two** of the cohort, the first time you run an event — almost always a [pickup](../systems/run-a-pickup) — as the coach in charge, with Sam present as a silent observer rather than a co-runner.

## Before

- **Design it and review it.** Even a pickup has a plan — the rotation, the format, the level mix. Walk it through [the Lesson-Plan Review Flow](lesson-plan-review-flow) with Sam in your [1:1](weekly-1-1-flow) beforehand. Nothing about your first event should be improvised.
- **Confirm the logistics yourself.** Court, equipment, players notified. Even though Sam's there, *you* own the [logistics](../systems/court-and-logistics) — being on top of them is half of what makes you feel ready.
- **Script your two moments.** The opening (greeting players, announcing the rotation — clear and confident) and the close (last round, thanks by name, next time). Nerves hit hardest at the start; a scripted open carries you through it.
- **Know it'll be imperfect.** It will be. That's the point — it's a rep, not a performance. The goal is to *run it and debrief it*, not to run it flawlessly.

## During

- **You are in charge.** Sam is observing, not co-running. The players should experience *you* as the coach. Own the room.
- **The silent-observer rule.** Sam will not interrupt your event — not to correct, not to rescue — unless there's a genuine safety issue. This is deliberate: interrupting would undercut your authority with the players and rob you of the rep. All of Sam's feedback comes *after*, in the debrief.
- **Run your fundamentals.** [Active Court](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars), the rotation, [reading the room](../frameworks/reading-the-room), the energy. You won't do all of it well. Do it.
- **When something goes sideways, adjust — don't freeze.** A lopsided game, an awkward number, a rotation hiccup. Reading the room and turning a dial is the skill; the wobble is normal.

## After

- **Run [the Post-Event Debrief Flow](post-event-debrief-flow) — you lead it.** Within ten minutes. Seven boxes, one thing that worked, one thing you'd change. You diagnose your own event first.
- **Then Sam adds.** Sam observed silently *so that* this debrief is rich. Sam goes second, after your self-diagnosis — questions first, then one note. The silent-observer rule and the coach-led debrief are two halves of the same design: your rep, your diagnosis, mentor input layered on top.

## The mindset

The first event feeling hard is the system working, not failing. You're meant to be slightly over your depth in a low-stakes setting with a mentor watching — that gap is exactly where the learning is. Run it, debrief it honestly, and the second one is already easier.

> Design it, review it with Sam, script your open and close. Then run it — you're in charge, Sam's silent. Debrief it yourself first. Imperfect is expected; the rep is the point.
