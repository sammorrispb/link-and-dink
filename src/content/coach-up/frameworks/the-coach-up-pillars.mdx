# The Coach Up Pillars

*Two pillars hold up every session you'll ever run. They're simple to state and hard to do — which is exactly why they're the standard. Get both, and almost everything else is detail. Miss one, and no amount of detail saves the session.*

---

## Why two pillars

A new coach can drown in things to think about — drills, technique, court management, the player who's frustrated, the one who's bored. The pillars cut through that. When you don't know if a session is working, you don't audit twenty things. You audit two:

1. **Active Court** — is everyone *doing*?
2. **Feedback Density** — is everyone *being coached*?

A session with both is a good session, even if it was rough around the edges. A polished session missing one is a session that quietly failed.

---

## Pillar 1 — Active Court

> **Every player, every minute: hitting, moving, or thinking about the next ball.**

The enemy here is the idle body. A player standing in a line, waiting for a turn, watching someone else hit — that player is not learning, not having fun, and quietly doing the math on whether this was worth their evening.

### The standard

There are **no passive bodies on a Coach Up court**. "No one idle more than a minute" is the floor, not the goal. The goal is a court where every player is inside a rally, in a constraint that demands attention, or actively reading a game they're about to rotate into.

### What it asks of you

- **Design out the lines.** If your session has a moment where three players watch one player hit, you built it wrong. Small-sided games, parallel courts, partner work — there is always a format that keeps everyone in it.
- **Make the rest deliberate.** Adults sometimes *do* need a breather — a tweaky knee, a hot day, an older player pacing themselves. That's fine. The rule isn't "never stop"; it's "rest is a decision, not an accident." A designed water break is active-court-positive. A player drifting off because the drill forgot about them is not.
- **Keep the ratios honest.** The more players per coach, the harder this pillar gets. Use formats that are self-sustaining — games with built-in rotations, constraints that don't need you to feed every ball. You are not the engine of the court. The format is.

### The adult adjustment

With kids, "active" is mostly physical. With adults, **thinking counts as active** — a player off-court for one rotation who's genuinely reading the game, picking up the pattern, ready to apply it when they rotate in, is active. A player off-court checking their phone is not. Your job is to make the off-court moment a coaching moment, not dead time.

## Pillar 2 — Feedback Density

> **Every player, every couple of minutes, by name.**

A player who goes ten minutes without hearing their name and one specific, useful thing is a player who is, functionally, practicing alone. They could have done that without you.

### The standard

In a typical small-group session, every player should get a feedback touch **every two to five minutes** — by name, specific, tied to what they're working on. Over a 60-minute session that's dozens of touches per player. That math only works if you understand the next part.

### Reinforcement is feedback

Most new coaches think "feedback" means "correction" — so they go quiet until something breaks. That guarantees you'll miss the standard, and it builds a court where the only attention a player gets is when they fail.

**Reinforcement counts.** "Yes — that's the reset." "Good, you were ready that time." "That's the one." Catching a good rep and naming it the instant it happens is feedback, it's the feedback that builds a player who comes back, and it's what makes the density math possible. (The full model is in [The Feedback Framework](the-feedback-framework).)

### What it asks of you

- **Know every name by the end of the first session.** Non-negotiable. Feedback "by name" is the whole pillar.
- **Stay loud and in the moment.** Audible feedback during play, longer teaching at the natural breaks. Never whispered, never saved up for the end.
- **Spread it.** New coaches feed the player they find most interesting — the one improving fastest, or the one struggling most. Density means *everyone*. Run your eyes around the court on a rotation.

### The adult adjustment

Adults are more self-conscious about correction and more responsive to being *seen*. Lean even harder on reinforcement than you would with kids — and when you do correct, the relationship and the reinforcement you've banked are what let the correction land without making it weird.

## How the pillars work together

They're not a checklist — they **compound**. An active court gives you more reps to give feedback on. Dense feedback makes the active reps actually teach something. Each pillar makes the other one worth more.

They can also fight each other if you let them. The classic failure: a coach stops live play to deliver feedback. That's Feedback Density attacking Active Court — you bought one teaching moment by freezing the whole room. Don't. Feedback rides *alongside* active play (audible, in the moment) or waits for a natural break. (This is Operating Principle "never stop live play to correct" — see [The Operating Principles](../creed/operating-principles).)

```
ACTIVE COURT  ──────────►  more reps to coach
      ▲                          │
      │                          ▼
  better reps  ◄──────  FEEDBACK DENSITY
```

## The pillar audit

After any session, you can grade yourself in one question per pillar:

- **Active Court:** *Was there any moment a player was idle by accident?*
- **Feedback Density:** *Could every player tell me what they worked on and one thing I said about it?*

Two honest answers. That's the whole audit. The full version is [The Apprentice Self-Assessment](the-apprentice-self-assessment).

> Two pillars. Everyone doing, everyone coached. Get those, and you're already a better coach than most of the people teaching this sport.
