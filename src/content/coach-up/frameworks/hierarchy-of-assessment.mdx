# The Hierarchy of Assessment

*If you internalize one framework in this whole handbook, make it this one. It's how you read a player and decide what to actually fix — instead of fixing the first thing you see.*

---

## The core idea

A player hits a shot. It goes wrong. There is a visible symptom — almost always the swing — and there are four possible causes underneath it. Most coaches see the symptom and immediately coach the swing. They're right maybe a third of the time, and the other two-thirds they're teaching a fix that can't stick because it's aimed at the wrong level.

The Hierarchy protects against that. When a shot breaks, you **walk four levels in order** and fix at the level that actually broke:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                            │
│   1. INTENT      What were they TRYING to do?              │
│                                                            │
│   2. OUTCOME     Did the shot DO what they wanted?         │
│                                                            │
│   3. TECHNIQUE   Is the physical execution off?            │
│                                                            │
│   4. TACTICS     Was it the right shot for the situation?  │
│                                                            │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**In order. No skipping.** Almost every coaching mistake is jumping straight to Technique without confirming Intent and Outcome first.

---

## Level 1 — Intent: *What were they trying to do?*

Before you correct anything, ask the player — out loud, genuinely:

> **"What were you going for there?"**

If the player can't answer, **stop**. No technical fix will hold, because you'd be teaching them to execute a shot they weren't actually trying to hit. The fix at this level isn't a stroke — it's teaching the player to *have an intent before every ball*. That's a skill of its own, and for a lot of adult rec players it's the single highest-leverage thing you'll ever teach them.

**Adult note:** adults will often back-fill an intent to sound competent — "oh, I was going for the drop" — when they actually just swung. Watch the shot, not just the answer. If the body didn't commit to a drop, the intent wasn't really there.

---

## Level 2 — Outcome: *Did the shot do what they wanted?*

This level is about the scoreboard, not the aesthetics.

> *An ugly reset that lands in the kitchen is a success. A picture-perfect drive into the net is not.*

### In practice

- A clunky-looking reset that still drops into the opponent's kitchen → **good outcome.** Do not rebuild the stroke. Reinforce it: "Ugly, and it worked — that's a reset."
- A gorgeous drive that sails long → **bad outcome.** The swing isn't the problem. The trajectory or the pace is.

If intent was clear and outcome matched it — you're done. Reinforce and move on. If intent was clear and outcome *didn't* match, **now** you drop to Technique.

---

## Level 3 — Technique: *Is the physical execution off?*

Grip, contact point, paddle face, swing path, footwork, balance. This is where most coaches *start*. The Hierarchy is what stops you from starting here — you only arrive at Technique once Intent and Outcome are confirmed.

### Coach where the ball goes, not how the arm moves

When you do coach technique, the research is clear (see [Research Foundations](../creed/research-foundations) — Wulf, OPTIMAL theory): an **external cue** — pointing at the effect in the world — beats an **internal cue** — pointing at the body.

| Internal (slower to learn) | External (faster, holds under pressure) |
| --- | --- |
| "Bend your knees more" | "Get lower under the ball" |
| "Close your paddle face" | "Aim for their feet" |
| "Brush up the back of the ball" | "Make it curl down into the kitchen" |
| "Firm wrist" | "Punch a hole in the wall behind them" |

Default to external. Reach for an internal cue only when nothing external is landing — and then get back to external as fast as you can.

---

## Level 4 — Tactics: *Was it the right shot?*

The top of the Hierarchy, because **a perfectly executed wrong shot still loses the point.**

### In practice

- A textbook drive from the kitchen line while both opponents are already at the net → technique is fine, **tactics are wrong.** That should have been a dink.
- A beautiful lob over an opponent who's already back at the baseline → technique is fine, **tactics are wrong.** Nothing to lob over.

The go-to question: *"Right shot, wrong shot — which was that?"* Tactics come last because a player can't meaningfully *choose* shots until Intent and Outcome are already working.

---

## The phrases you'll actually say

| Level | Go-to line |
| --- | --- |
| Intent | "What were you going for there?" |
| Outcome | "Did that do what you wanted?" |
| Technique | "We fix the *how* after the *what* and the *why*." |
| Tactics | "Right shot, wrong shot — which was that?" |

---

## How to practice the Hierarchy

This is a skill, and it sharpens with deliberate reps. Three drills on yourself:

**One player, one session.** Pick one player. For the whole session, diagnose *only* through the Hierarchy. After every miss of theirs, internally run all four levels. You'll be startled how often the honest answer is "they had no intent at all."

**The before-any-correction rule.** For one full session, don't make a single technical correction without first asking intent out loud. It's hard and humbling and it permanently changes how you coach.

**Peer observation.** Watch a cohort-mate coach and silently run the Hierarchy on three of their diagnostic moments. Did they skip Intent? Jump to Technique? Compare notes after. (This is built into the [12-week roadmap](../curriculum/roadmap-overview).)

## The big reminder

> **Before you fix the stroke, ask: did they do what they meant to?**

If a new coach internalizes one sentence from this entire handbook — that's the one.
