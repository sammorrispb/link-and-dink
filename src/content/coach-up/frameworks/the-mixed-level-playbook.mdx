# The Mixed-Level Playbook

*A youth academy can hand-pick level-matched groups at an evaluation. You usually can't. In adult rec coaching — pickups, socials, most clinics — a spread of levels on one court is the* normal *condition. This playbook is how you make that work instead of letting it quietly break the session.*

---

## Why this is its own framework

The seed material treats mixed levels as a problem to solve and route around. For Coach Up, that's the wrong frame. The eight players who show up to a Tuesday pickup are whoever showed up — a 2.5 next to a 4.0 is Tuesday, not an emergency. **Mixed-level is a design constraint to coach within, not a failure to apologize for.**

Get it wrong and two things break at once:
- The **stronger players** get bored — under-challenged, going through the motions, doing the math on whether to come back.
- The **weaker players** get exposed — overmatched, self-conscious, the silent center of attention.

Both of those are retention failures. The playbook exists to prevent both — usually inside one game, on one court.

---

## The core move: change the player, not the game

The instinct of a new coach facing a mixed court is to split it — strong court, weak court. Sometimes that's right (see below). But it's the *last* tool, not the first, because splitting halves your attention and segregates the room socially, and the social layer is half of why adults are there.

The first tool is the **Individual dial** from [The Constraint Design Toolkit](the-constraint-design-toolkit): keep one game, one court, one set of rules — and give different players different *personal* constraints inside it.

Everyone is playing the same cross-court dink game. But:
- the 4.0 has to do it off their backhand only,
- the 3.5 has to do it to a target half the kitchen,
- the 3.0 just has to keep ten in a row,
- the 2.5 gets a fed, predictable feed and a count of five.

Same game. Same court. Same social experience. Four genuinely different challenges. Nobody's bored, nobody's exposed, and from the outside it just looks like one game running.

---

## The five tools, in order of reach

Reach for these roughly in order — invisible adjustments first, structural splits last.

### 1. Personal constraints (the Individual dial)

As above. Your default and your most-used tool. Invisible, social-positive, and it scales to almost any spread. Master this one and you rarely need the rest.

### 2. Pairing strategy

Who's with whom is a design decision. Two reliable patterns:
- **Bridge pairing** — pair across the gap (strong + developing) for *cooperative* work. The stronger player gets a "feed and challenge them" role, the developing player gets steady, makeable balls. Both are working.
- **Peer pairing** — pair within a level for *competitive* work, where a real contest needs to be roughly even.
Match the pairing to whether the block is cooperative or competitive.

### 3. Handicap formats for competitive play

When the block is competitive and the gap is real, build the handicap into the scoring, not into who plays whom:
- the stronger side starts down a few points,
- the stronger player can only score off a designated shot,
- the developing side gets a "safe zone" the stronger player can't attack.
A handicapped game is a *real* game for both sides. An un-handicapped mismatch is a real game for neither.

### 4. The rotation as a leveler

In a group or pickup, design the rotation so the spread is constantly re-mixed — nobody gets stuck for forty minutes either over- or under-matched. A rotation that re-pairs every few minutes turns a wide level spread from a problem into variety. ([Run a Pickup](../systems/run-a-pickup) covers rotation design in depth.)

### 5. The temporary split — last, and on purpose

Sometimes the gap is genuinely too wide for one game — a true beginner in a 4.0 group. Then you split, but do it *deliberately and temporarily*: a focused block on one court for the player who needs different reps, then bring the room back together for live play and the social close. A split should be a chapter of the session, not the architecture of it.

## Reading a mixed room

Your [Reading the Room](reading-the-room) tells fire constantly in a mixed session. The two to watch hardest:

- **The bored strong player** — under-challenged, often starts over-coaching the others or going quiet. Move: tighten their personal constraint, or give them a real cooperative role. Do not let them drift; a bored 4.0 is a 4.0 who found a better Tuesday.
- **The small, quiet weak player** — ego-protecting, overmatched. Move: loosen their personal constraint until they get a genuine winnable rep, re-pair them onto a bridge, and bank private reinforcement. Never let them be the silent center of attention.

---

## What you tell the players

Adults are doing a cost-benefit calculation, and a mixed court can read as "I'm being short-changed" to either end. Get ahead of it — name it, briefly, in [the frame](session-design-framework):

> *"We've got a range of levels today, and that's on purpose — everyone's going to be working on something a little different inside the same games. If it ever feels too easy or too hard, that's information for me, tell me."*

That one sentence reframes a mixed court from a compromise into a feature, and it turns every player into a sensor that helps you read the room.

> Mixed-level is Tuesday, not an emergency. Change the player, not the game — give everyone a different challenge inside the same court. Split only when the gap genuinely can't be designed around, and only as a chapter, never the whole story.
