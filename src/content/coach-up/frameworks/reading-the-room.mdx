# Reading the Room

*[Session Design](session-design-framework) is the plan. This is the "hold it loosely" half — reading what's actually happening in front of you and adjusting the design while the session is still moving. It's the hardest skill in coaching, and the most learnable, because it's just reps plus a checklist of tells.*

---

## What you're reading

You designed a session. The design is *slightly wrong* — it always is. Too hard, too easy, repping the wrong thing for this group on this day. Reading the room is catching that gap in real time and closing it with a dial-turn, without stopping the court.

You're reading three things at once:

1. **The group's energy** — is the court alive, or has it gone flat?
2. **The individual** — is *this* player engaged, productively struggling, or quietly checked out?
3. **The design's fit** — is the constraint producing the productive failure you wanted, or collapse, or boredom?

---

## The tells — and what each one means

These are the signals. Each one points at a likely cause and a likely move.

| What you see | What it usually means | The move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **The court goes quiet** | Disengagement. A working court has noise. | Inject energy, or change the game — the current one stopped landing. |
| **A player looks bored** | Wrong level — almost always *under*-challenged. Boredom is a diagnostic, not a discipline problem. | Turn the [Individual dial](the-constraint-design-toolkit): a harder personal constraint inside the same game. |
| **A player is visibly frustrated** | The constraint collapsed — they're failing every rep, not productively. | Pull your off-ramp. Loosen a dial. Get them a winnable rep *now*. |
| **A player goes quiet and small** | Ego protection — an adult who feels exposed. The biggest retention risk on the court. | Get them a private, genuine reinforcement. Re-pair them. Never leave them the silent center of attention. |
| **The rally never starts** | The live-ball constraint is too tight — chaos, no reps. | Feed the first ball yourself, or widen the target, until rallies sustain. |
| **One player is over-talking / over-coaching others** | Often a strong player who's under-challenged and filling the space. | Challenge them (Individual dial) or give them a real role — but keep them off your coaching turf. |
| **Energy sags ~40 minutes in** | Normal fatigue, especially with older adults. | A designed break, a lighter/funnier game, or shift to live play. Don't push a tired room through precision work. |
| **Players keep glancing at you between every rep** | Dependence — they're coaching off your face, not reading the game. | Go quiet on purpose. Let the [game be the feedback](the-feedback-framework). Make yourself less necessary. |

## The adult-specific reads

Adults hide things kids don't. Three reads that matter more with an adult room:

- **Politeness masks boredom.** A bored adult won't act out — they'll smile and disengage. So you can't wait for disruption as your signal. Watch the *body*: feet slowing down, less recovery between balls, eyes drifting. A polite adult will let a bad session happen to them and simply not come back.
- **Ego masks frustration.** An adult failing reps will often go quiet and self-deprecating ("I'm hopeless at this") rather than show anger. That self-deprecation is a flare. It means *engineer a win immediately* — it is not a personality trait to laugh along with.
- **"I'm fine" usually isn't.** When you check in and an adult deflects, believe the body over the words. Re-read, adjust the design, and let the next few reps tell you the truth.

## How to adjust without stopping the court

The amateur move is to stop the whole session, gather everyone, and re-explain. That kills [Active Court](the-coach-up-pillars) and announces "the plan failed." The craft move is to adjust *inside the flow*:

- **Turn one dial.** Reading the room tells you *which* dial; [the Toolkit](the-constraint-design-toolkit) tells you *how*. One turn, then re-read.
- **Adjust at the natural break.** A constraint change can usually wait the ten seconds for a rally to end. Announce it like it was always the plan — "okay, next round, target shrinks to the cross-court half."
- **Use the Individual dial silently.** The most invisible adjustment: walk to one player and change *their* constraint without touching the group's game. Nobody else even knows you read something.
- **When in doubt, loosen.** A new coach's instinct under stress is to add structure and explanation. Usually the room needs *less* — a simpler constraint, a winnable rep, a moment of play. Loosen first; you can always tighten back up.

## How this skill actually develops

Reading the room feels like instinct in a great coach. It isn't — it's pattern recognition built from reps plus reflection. You build it three ways, all in the [12-week roadmap](../curriculum/roadmap-overview):

1. **Coach the reps.** Every session you run is a session of reading practice.
2. **Debrief honestly.** "What did I miss, and when?" The [post-event debrief](../flows/post-event-debrief-flow) is where a missed read becomes a learned tell.
3. **Watch other coaches.** Observing a session with no responsibility — just reading the room as an exercise — is the fastest way to sharpen the eye. (Peer observation, built into the roadmap.)

> The plan gets you to the court. Reading the room is everything after that. Watch the bodies, not the words — and when you're not sure, turn one dial and loosen.
