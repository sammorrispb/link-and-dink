# The Adult Skill Progression

*A map of what to teach an adult rec player, and in what order. It is not a rigid ladder — you spiral back through it constantly — but the order of* introduction *matters, and most coaches get it backwards.*

---

## The two ordering principles

Two ideas decide the order, and both run against a new coach's instinct.

**Control before power.** A new coach's instinct is to teach the satisfying shots first — the drive, the put-away, the overhead. But at the rec level, points are overwhelmingly won by *consistency and control*, not power. Teach a player to keep the ball in play softly and they win games tomorrow. Teach them to hit hard first and you've built someone who looks good losing.

**Tactics before technique.** Following [the research](../creed/research-foundations) — the Hierarchy of Assessment, Teaching Games for Understanding — a player should understand the *problem a shot solves* before drilling the shot. So the progression is sequenced by *when a player needs the shot to play the game well*, not by what's easiest to demonstrate.

The result: a progression that starts with the platform, builds the soft game, and saves the chaotic, exciting stuff for last.

---

## The eight blocks

```
1. THE ATHLETIC BASE     → the platform everything else stands on
2. THE SOFT GAME         → control; where rec points are actually won
3. THE RESET             → survival; defense back to neutral
4. SERVE & RETURN        → every point starts here
5. THE THIRD SHOT        → the pivotal decision: drop or drive
6. THE TRANSITION        → getting through no-man's-land to the line
7. THE SPEED-UP GAME     → offense and hands battles — chaos, taught last
8. POINT CONSTRUCTION    → the integration block: choosing shots across a rally
```

### 1. The Athletic Base

Ready position, the split-step, balanced footwork, paddle up and in front. Boring to teach, and the foundation of everything — a player who isn't *set before the ball comes* can't execute any other block reliably. With adults, frame it as the thing that makes the rest of the game feel slower and easier. You will return to this block every single session.

### 2. The Soft Game

Dinks and kitchen-line play. This is block two on purpose: it's where rec-level points are won and lost, it's the most controllable part of the game, and it gives a nervous adult immediate, low-risk reps that build confidence. Cross-court dinks, dinking with patience, moving the opponent — the soft game is the spine of adult pickleball.

### 3. The Reset

The soft, defensive ball — from mid-court or under pressure — that drops into the opponent's kitchen and buys time to recover. It comes right after the soft game because it's the same soft hands applied under pressure. Teach it early: a player who can reset can *survive*, and survival is what keeps a rally alive long enough to learn anything else.

### 4. Serve & Return

Every point starts here, so a player needs both functional fast. The serve: deep, consistent, in — power comes much later. The return: **deep**, because a deep return buys the time to get to the kitchen line, which is the single most valuable positional habit in the game. Frame the return as a *positioning tool*, not a weapon.

### 5. The Third Shot

The drop and the drive — and, more importantly, the *decision between them*. This is the pivotal shot of pickleball and the highest-leverage thing you'll teach an intermediate adult. Use [the Hierarchy of Assessment](hierarchy-of-assessment) hard here: most third-shot problems are Intent problems (the player didn't decide drop-or-drive before the ball came), not technique problems.

### 6. The Transition

Moving through the mid-court — "no-man's-land" — from the baseline up to the kitchen line without getting punished. It comes after the third shot because the transition is what *happens after* a third-shot drop. Resets (block 3) are the survival tool that makes the transition possible.

### 7. The Speed-Up Game

Hands battles, attacking off a high ball, counter-attacking at the kitchen line. It's the most exciting part of the game and it's taught **last** of the skill blocks — not because it doesn't matter, but because it's the most chaotic, and a player needs the base, the soft game, and the reset already working before fast hands at the net are anything but a coin flip.

### 8. Point Construction

The integration block. Not a new shot — the skill of *choosing* shots across a whole rally: when to stay soft, when to speed up, when to reset, where to put the ball. This is [Hierarchy](hierarchy-of-assessment) Level 4 (Tactics) as a coaching focus, and it's the block that turns a player who can hit shots into a player who can *play*.

## How to actually use this

- **It's an order of introduction, not a gate.** You don't finish block 2 and lock it. You introduce in this order and then *spiral* — every session touches the base, most sessions touch the soft game, and you layer the later blocks on top as players are ready.
- **Diagnose to find the block.** When a player is stuck, the [Hierarchy](hierarchy-of-assessment) tells you which block they actually need — which is often earlier than the one they think they're working on. A player flailing at speed-ups (block 7) is very often missing the athletic base (block 1).
- **A session focus is usually one block.** When you design a session ([Session Design Framework](session-design-framework)), the focus is typically a single block from this list. That's what keeps a session coherent.
- **Adults will want to skip ahead.** They'll ask for the drive, the put-away, the fancy stuff. Honor the curiosity, give them a taste — then bring them back. The progression is the thing that actually makes the fancy stuff work.

> Control before power. Tactics before technique. Build the base, win the soft game, and teach the chaos last.
