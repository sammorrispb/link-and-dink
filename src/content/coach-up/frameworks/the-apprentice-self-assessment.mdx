# The Apprentice Self-Assessment

*Reps don't make you a coach. Reps plus honest reflection make you a coach. This is the reflection — a seven-box check you run on yourself within ten minutes of every session you coach, while it's still fresh.*

---

## Why it's on you

In a lot of coach development, assessment is something done *to* you — a supervisor's clipboard, a quarterly review. Coach Up runs it the other way. **You assess yourself, every session, immediately** — because self-diagnosis beats top-down correction, because the memory is freshest in the first ten minutes, and because a coach who can honestly grade their own session is a coach who keeps getting better without anyone standing over them.

This is not paperwork. It's the [Creed's belief #10](../creed/the-coach-up-creed) — *the coachable coach wins* — made into a habit.

---

## The seven boxes

Run these right after the session. Courtside, in your head or on your phone — not that night, not tomorrow.

```
☐  1. Every player was active — no one idle by accident.
☐  2. Every player got feedback every few minutes, by name.
☐  3. I reinforced more than I corrected.
☐  4. I diagnosed before I taught — I asked intent before fixing technique.
☐  5. I had a designed plan, and I adjusted at least one thing by reading the room.
☐  6. Every player can name what they worked on and one thing to keep doing.
☐  7. I ran my own debrief honestly — not the flattering version.
```

### What each box is really checking

1. **Active Court.** [Pillar one](the-coach-up-pillars). Idle bodies don't learn and don't come back.
2. **Feedback Density.** [Pillar two](the-coach-up-pillars). By name, every few minutes — reinforcement counts.
3. **Reinforce over correct.** [Creed belief #4](../creed/the-coach-up-creed). New coaches default to correction; this box fights that drift.
4. **Diagnose before teach.** The [Hierarchy of Assessment](hierarchy-of-assessment) actually used, not skipped.
5. **Plan, held loosely.** You designed a session *and* you read the room and flexed it. Both halves — a script you won't change and pure improv both miss this box.
6. **The learning landed.** The most important box. It's the only one that catches "I was active and busy but I didn't actually teach anything." A coach can hit every other box and still have players who can't name what they did.
7. **Honest debrief.** Did you run the real [post-event debrief](../flows/post-event-debrief-flow), or the comfortable one? This box is the integrity check on the other six.

---

## Box 6 is the one that matters most

You will be tempted, on a tiring day, to treat box 6 as a formality. Don't. It is the box that separates *activity* from *coaching*.

A session can be loud, busy, well-paced, and fun — every body moving, lots of feedback flying — and still teach nothing, because nothing was aimed. Box 6 forces the honest question: **if I asked any player right now what they worked on, could they tell me?** If the answer is no, the session was exercise. Good exercise, maybe. Not coaching.

When box 6 fails, the cause is almost always upstream: the session had no single focus, or the [frame](session-design-framework) didn't land, or you never closed with one-takeaway-each. Trace it back.

---

## The standard

You are not aiming for seven of seven every session — a perfect score usually means you graded yourself softly. The standard is:

- **Hit at least five of seven**, consistently, across a stretch of sessions.
- **Box 7 is non-negotiable** — you either ran an honest debrief or you didn't; there's no "mostly."
- **Watch your pattern.** One missed box once is noise. The *same* box missed for three sessions running is a signal — that's your next [1:1 with Sam](../flows/weekly-1-1-flow) topic, and it's exactly the kind of pattern the [Readiness Bar](../progression/the-readiness-bar) is looking at.

The score is never the point. The *pattern* is the point — it tells you, and Sam, where the craft is still thin.
