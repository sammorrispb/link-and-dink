# The Feedback Framework

*[The Hierarchy of Assessment](hierarchy-of-assessment) tells you* what *to fix. This framework tells you* how to say it *— so it actually lands. Feedback Density is one of [the two Pillars](the-coach-up-pillars); this is the craft underneath it.*

---

## The three layers — all of them count

New coaches think "feedback" means "correction." It doesn't. There are three layers, and **all three count toward [Feedback Density](the-coach-up-pillars)** — the standard math only works because reinforcement counts.

| Layer | What it sounds like | When you use it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Reinforce** | "Yes — that's the reset." "Good, you were ready that time." | Every time a player does the thing. The instant it happens. |
| **Introduce** | "Try getting lower under the next one." | A new skill, or a new wrinkle on an old one. |
| **Correct** | "Reset that — softer hands." | An off-target rep, once intent and outcome are confirmed. |

Most coaches live in **Correct**. Great Coach Up coaches live in **Reinforce** — not because correction doesn't matter, but because a court where the only feedback is correction is a court that's slowly emptying. Reinforce is also how you bank the relationship that lets a correction land later without making it weird.

> If you can't find a rep to reinforce, your task was set too hard. That's a design problem, not a player problem — fix the constraint, not the player.

---

## The four modalities — say it more than one way

The same point can be delivered through four channels. Use more than one; players don't all receive the same way.

- **Verbal** — audible, constant, in the moment. Your default. The running soundtrack of an active court.
- **Demo** — show it. Once, cleanly, at the start of a block or when a verbal cue isn't landing. Don't perform; demonstrate, then get them hitting.
- **Peer** — point one player at another's good rep. "Watch how she was set before the ball came — do that." Adults learn well from a peer who's one level up.
- **Game-built-in** — the strongest modality, and the most overlooked. A well-designed game *is* feedback: if the constraint is "the point only counts if the third shot lands in the kitchen," the game tells the player whether they did it — you didn't have to say a word. (This is the whole point of [The Constraint Design Toolkit](the-constraint-design-toolkit).)

Reach for the situational ones when they fit: a gentle, consent-based physical guide for a stuck paddle face; a phone video for a mechanic that's hard to feel. Use them as tools, not crutches.

## The timing rule — when feedback happens

This is the rule that keeps Feedback Density from attacking [Active Court](the-coach-up-pillars):

- **During live play** → short, audible, in-the-moment cues only. "Paddle up." "Nice drop." "Reset it." One or two words. They ride alongside the rally; they don't stop it.
- **At natural breaks** → the longer teaching moment. End of a rally, end of a point, block transition, water break. This is where a real correction or a new concept goes.

**Never stop live play to correct.** Stopping play to teach feels like teaching — it's the moment you lose the room, and it kills the active court to buy one teaching moment. Wait three seconds for the rally to end. The break is always coming.

## The one-note discipline

The single most common new-coach feedback failure is **trying to fix everything at once.** A player misses a third shot; the coach sees the grip, the footwork, the contact point, the shot choice — and says all four. None of them land. The player walks away with mush.

**One coaching note per teaching moment.** Pick the biggest lever — the level lowest on the [Hierarchy](hierarchy-of-assessment) that's actually broken — and coach only that. The other three are real, and they'll still be there next rep. Mush helps no one; one clear thing changes a player.

This applies to your debriefs of yourself, too — see [the post-event debrief flow](../flows/post-event-debrief-flow).

## The adult adjustments

- **Lead with the why.** An adult takes a cue better when they know the problem it solves. "Get lower — it's what lets you lift the ball softly instead of swinging at it" beats a bare "get lower."
- **Reinforce harder than you would with a kid.** Adults are more self-conscious about the bad reps and more responsive to being genuinely seen on the good ones.
- **Specific, not sycophantic.** "Tight reset — you bought yourself the whole next shot" is feedback. "AMAZING!!" is noise that the adult will correctly discount. Warm and exact, never inflated.
- **Honest, delivered warm.** The warmest thing you can do for an adult player is tell them the true, useful thing in a way they can hear. Vague niceness is how coaches dodge the actual job.

---

## The framework in one line

> Three layers, all counting. Four ways to say it. In the moment or at the break — never mid-rally. And only ever **one note at a time.**
