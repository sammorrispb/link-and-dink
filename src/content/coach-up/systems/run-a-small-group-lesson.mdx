# How to Run a Small-Group Lesson

*Two to four players, real teaching, one coach. It's the format that tests whether you can hold [both Pillars](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars) at once — and the format where most adult coaching actually happens.*

---

## The arc

It runs the [standard session arc](../frameworks/session-design-framework) — warm and read, the frame, skill blocks, live application, one takeaway each — at full size. The shape is the same as a 1:1; what changes is that you now have to deliver it to three or four players *simultaneously*.

## What the small group demands

- **The Pillars get genuinely hard here.** With one player, [Active Court](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars) and [Feedback Density](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars) are easy. With four, you have to *design* for them: games with no lines, formats that keep everyone hitting, and a deliberate habit of running your eyes around the group so feedback reaches *all* of them, not just the most interesting one.
- **Mixed level is the default.** Four adults who signed up together are rarely the same level. [The Mixed-Level Playbook](../frameworks/the-mixed-level-playbook) isn't optional reading for this format — it's the operating manual. Change the player, not the game: personal constraints inside one shared game.
- **The frame serves everyone.** One focus, framed so its *why* lands for the whole group — even though each player will work on it at a slightly different depth.
- **Feedback has to spread.** The single most common small-group failure: the coach feeds the player they find most interesting and three others get coached half as much. Density means *everyone*. Spread it on purpose.
- **Track a running focus per player.** Harder than in a 1:1, and just as important. Each player in a recurring group still has their own through-line; a quick note per player after each session keeps the group from flattening into a generic class.

## Common failures

- **Lines.** The default small-group failure mode — three players watching one hit. If your design has a line in it, it's wrong. [The Drill & Game Library](the-drill-and-game-library) is full of formats that don't.
- **Uneven feedback.** Coaching the standout and neglecting the rest. Run your eyes around the group every rotation.
- **Coaching to the average.** Treating four players as one generic player — the strong one's bored, the new one's lost. Personal constraints fix this.
- **Losing the through-line.** No running focus per player, so the group never compounds.

## Where it fits

The small-group lesson is, for most adult coaches, the *core* of the business — better economics per hour than a 1:1, more real teaching than an event. It's also the hardest test of whether you can run the [Pillars](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars) and the [Mixed-Level Playbook](../frameworks/the-mixed-level-playbook) at the same time, live. Master this format and most of the rest of the craft is in reach.

> Same arc as a 1:1, four times the read. Design out the lines, spread the feedback, change the player not the game — and keep a through-line on every one of them.
