# Pricing & Selling

*Coaching is a craft, but it's also a small business — and pricing is where most new coaches quietly undercut themselves. This is how to price like a professional and sell without being a salesperson.*

---

## Build a rate card

Your first move is a [rate card](../vocabulary/term-rate-card) — a clear, written menu of what you offer and what it costs: 1:1s, small groups, clinics, any packages. Written down, decided once.

A coach without a rate card improvises a number every time someone asks. That reads as amateur, it invites negotiation, and it trains clients to expect discounts. A coach *with* one quotes the same confident number every time.

## The pricing principles

- **Don't underprice out of nerves.** The single most common new-coach error is pricing the first lessons low because charging real money feels uncomfortable. Don't. Price fairly and *over-deliver*. A discount is hard to walk back; a reputation for value is not.
- **Discounts train clients to wait for discounts.** Hold your rate. If you want to be generous, be generous with the *coaching* — an extra ten minutes, a follow-up note — not the price.
- **Price the value, not the hour.** You're not selling sixty minutes of court time. You're selling diagnosis, a designed session, a through-line, and real progress. Price what it's worth.
- **Packages reward commitment.** A multi-session package — priced as a modest commitment incentive, not a desperation discount — is good business and good coaching, because development needs a series, not a one-off.
- **Revisit it as you climb.** Your rate isn't fixed forever. As your craft and your results grow — as you move up [the Coach Up ladder](../progression/the-coach-up-ladder) — your rate card grows with them.

## Know the rev-share structure

Coach Up's [rev-share](../vocabulary/term-rev-share) is simple and it shapes your pricing: L&D takes a share **only on events** where it's covering the court, on a scale that slides with the court cost. Your **lessons and clinics are 100% yours.** So:

- Your [rate card](../vocabulary/term-rate-card) — lessons and clinics — is entirely your revenue. Price it as your own business.
- Event pricing factors in the court cost and the L&D share. A free public court means you keep the event's fees; a premium indoor court L&D pays for means a larger share to L&D.

## Selling without being sales-y

The word "selling" makes good coaches flinch. Reframe it: **selling is just honestly communicating fit.**

- Lead with *their* goal, not your features. The [intake conversation](intake-and-rating-literacy) already told you why they're here — speak to that.
- Be specific and confident about what you offer and what it costs. Vagueness reads as a lack of conviction.
- Be honest about fit. If you're not the right coach for someone, say so. That honesty is what makes your *yes* trustworthy.
- No pressure, no hype. The L&D voice — direct, specific, no sales-y inflation — is also the right sales voice. ("It's mentorship, not certification" is a sales line that works *because* it's plain.)

## Common failures

- **No rate card** — improvising prices, looking amateur, inviting negotiation.
- **Underpricing** — the nerves discount that's hard to undo.
- **Discounting to close** — training every future client to expect it.
- **Selling features instead of fit** — talking about your drills instead of their goal.

> Write the rate card. Price the value, not the hour. Hold the number, be generous with the coaching — and sell by honestly communicating fit, not by pressure.
