# How to Run a Pot Night

*Pot Night is Link & Dink's signature competitive event. It's structured, it has stakes, and it runs on the night — which means running it well is mostly about being organized and being a calm, fair authority.*

---

## The format

Eight players, doubles. A round robin to seed, then a single-elimination bracket. A small entry fee per player; the pot is paid out to the winners the same night. It's competitive but friendly — the appeal is *real games with real stakes* against a known group, not a cutthroat tournament.

As the coach running it, you are the **tournament director** for the night: you run the bracket, keep it on time, settle the calls, and pay it out.

## The arc

```
CHECK-IN            Confirm the eight, collect entries, confirm the pot.
(first 10 min)      Everyone knows the format before a ball is hit.

ROUND ROBIN         Seeding games. You run the schedule, keep courts
                    moving, track results visibly.

THE BRACKET         Single elimination off the seeding. Announce
                    matchups clearly. Keep it moving.

THE PAYOUT          Winners paid on the night, in front of everyone.
& CLOSE             Thank players by name. Name the next one.
```

## The decisions you own

- **Stay on time.** A Pot Night that drags loses the room. Know your schedule, call matches promptly, keep courts turning over. Time management *is* the job here.
- **Be the authority on calls.** Line calls, scoring disputes — players will look to you. Be calm, fair, decisive, and consistent. You don't have to be right about everything; you have to be steady and even-handed.
- **Keep it friendly.** Stakes can tighten people up. Your tone sets the room's tone — competitive *and* warm. Read the room ([Reading the Room](../frameworks/reading-the-room)) for anyone getting too tight, and lighten it.
- **Handle the money cleanly.** Entries in, pot out, transparent, same night. Players are trusting you with it — be visibly organized and exact. Nothing erodes trust faster than a fuzzy payout.

## Common failures

- **Losing the schedule.** No clear bracket, matches called late, players unsure who's up — the night unravels. Have the structure written down and visible.
- **Inconsistent calls.** Ruling one way early and another way later. Pick a standard, state it, hold it.
- **Letting it get tense.** Forgetting that "friendly" is half the product. The stakes are the hook; the warmth is the retention.
- **Sloppy money handling.** Be exact and transparent, every time.

## Where it fits

Pot Night is a *Coach Up* event type and an L&D product — when you run one, you're representing the brand directly. It's higher-structure than [a pickup](run-a-pickup) and lower-coaching — there's almost no teaching here, it's pure event management. That makes it a great rep for the *organizer* half of the craft. Logistics detail lives in [Court & Logistics](court-and-logistics).

> On Pot Night you're the tournament director: organized, on time, fair on every call, clean with the money — and warm enough that the stakes stay fun.
