# How to Design a Clinic

*A clinic is a small-group lesson scaled up and themed — more players, a named topic, a marketing promise. It's the format that most rewards real design and most punishes improvisation.*

---

## What a clinic is

A larger group session — often six to twelve players — built around a *named theme*: "The Third Shot Clinic," "Dinking & the Soft Game," "Transition Zone." Players sign up for the theme. That sign-up is a promise, and the clinic's whole job is to keep it.

The word in the title is *design*, not *run*. A clinic that's improvised is a basket of balls with a flyer. A clinic that's designed is a curriculum compressed into ninety minutes.

## Designing it

Run [the Session Design Framework](../frameworks/session-design-framework), scaled up:

- **One theme, honestly delivered.** The theme is usually one block of [the Adult Skill Progression](../frameworks/the-adult-skill-progression). Pick it, and make sure every minute of the clinic actually serves it — players paid for the thing on the flyer.
- **A climbing sequence of stations or blocks.** With a larger group, you'll often run *stations* or rotating blocks. Sequence them easy to hard, each one repping the theme, each one a step up.
- **Designed for the level spread.** A clinic's sign-up sheet doesn't filter for level. Assume a spread and build [the Mixed-Level Playbook](../frameworks/the-mixed-level-playbook) in from the start — personal constraints, bridge pairings, station design that flexes.
- **A frame that delivers the why — to a bigger room.** Same [frame](../vocabulary/term-the-frame) discipline, projected to twelve people. Short, clear, sells the theme's value.
- **A real close.** Every player leaves able to name what the clinic taught them and one thing to take home. At clinic scale this takes deliberate design — it won't happen by accident.

## Running it

- **Hold the Pillars at scale.** [Active Court](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars) with twelve people means stations and self-sustaining games — no lines, ever. [Feedback Density](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars) means a deliberate circuit of the room so everyone gets named, coached touches.
- **Manage time hard.** A clinic has a lot of moving parts and a fixed end. Know your station timings; keep it moving.
- **Recruit help if the numbers demand it.** Past a certain size, one coach can't hold the Pillars alone. Knowing when to bring a second pair of hands is part of the design.

## Common failures

- **Improvising it.** The cardinal sin. A clinic is a designed curriculum or it's nothing.
- **Drifting off the theme.** Players paid for the flyer's promise. Deliver exactly that.
- **Lines at scale.** Twelve people and a one-at-a-time drill is a disaster. Stations and game-based formats only.
- **No close.** A big group disperses fast — if you didn't design the one-takeaway-each moment, it didn't happen.

## Where it fits

A clinic is strong economics and strong marketing — a themed clinic is something you can *promote*, and it's a natural on-ramp to your [1:1s](structure-a-1-1-lesson) and [small groups](run-a-small-group-lesson). Under [rev-share](../vocabulary/term-rev-share), clinic revenue is yours. It's also the format that most demands you be a *designer*, not a ball-feeder — which is exactly the muscle Coach Up is built to grow.

> A clinic is a curriculum, not a class. Pick one theme, design the climb, build the level spread in from the start, and deliver exactly what the flyer promised.
