# How to Run a Social

*The social is the front door. It's the event you point a nervous newcomer at, the one that's about belonging before ball-striking. Run it well and it feeds every other thing you do.*

---

## What a social is

A deliberately low-pressure event built around *connection*. The pickleball is real, but it's the medium, not the point — the point is that people meet, mix, laugh, and leave with a reason to come back and a face they recognize. Think wide-open rotations, mixers, light formats, room to actually talk.

This format leans directly on [Coaching Adults vs. Kids](../creed/coaching-adults-vs-kids), shift #6: for a lot of adult rec players, the social layer isn't a bonus — it's a primary reason they play. The social is that reason, made into an event.

## What makes it work

- **Maximum mixing.** The rotation's job here isn't competitive balance — it's introductions. Design it so people play *with* and *against* as many others as possible. Nobody stuck with the same three people all night.
- **A genuinely warm host.** You are not the tournament director tonight. You're the host. Names, introductions, easing the nervous person in, making sure nobody's standing alone. This is the skill the social trains.
- **Formats that allow talking.** Choose games and rotations with built-in breathing room — the conversation between points is a feature, not lost time.
- **Zero exposure.** No standings, no stakes, no spotlight on anyone's level. A social where someone felt judged is a social that failed.

## The decisions you own

- **The rotation** — built for breadth of connection, not competitive fairness.
- **The temperature** — you keep it light, inclusive, easy. Read the room ([Reading the Room](../frameworks/reading-the-room)) hard for anyone on the edge of the group and pull them in.
- **The newcomer's experience** — the social is often someone's *first* event. Their first 30 minutes are the whole ballgame for whether they come back. Own that.
- **Almost no coaching** — a cue or a reinforcement if it's wanted, nothing more. Tonight you host; you don't teach.

## Common failures

- **Running it like a pickup.** A competitive rotation in a social setting misses the entire point.
- **Hosting passively.** Standing courtside isn't hosting. Hosting is active — circulating, introducing, including.
- **Letting cliques set the rotation.** Friends will want to stay together; the social's job is gentle, friendly mixing.
- **Coaching when nobody asked.** Tonight, restraint is the skill.

## Where it fits

The social is the widest part of the funnel — the lowest-commitment way for someone to enter your world. A great social sends players toward your [pickups](run-a-pickup), [ladders](run-a-ladder), and lessons. It's a [retention and acquisition](getting-and-keeping-students) tool disguised as a fun night, and it's where you build the host skills the warmer half of coaching runs on.

> A social is connection first, competition a distant second. Tonight you're the host, not the coach — and the newcomer's first half-hour is the whole job.
