# Intake & Rating Literacy

*The work before the first lesson. Done well, intake sets up everything that follows; done badly, you're coaching a stranger toward a goal you guessed at.*

---

## The intake conversation

Before a new student's first session, have a short, real conversation — in person, by phone, by message. You're after four things:

- **Why they're here.** The actual goal. "Win more," "stop getting hurt," "keep up with my friends' group," "feel less lost out there." This is the *why* you'll frame every session against. It's also rarely "improve my third shot drop" — that's a means; find the end.
- **Their history.** How long they've played, what other sports they bring, any past coaching. This tells you what habits and analogies you're working with ([Coaching Adults vs. Kids](../creed/coaching-adults-vs-kids), shift #2).
- **Their constraints.** Time, body, schedule, budget. An honest read here keeps you from designing a plan they can't actually run.
- **Their self-assessment.** What *they* think their level and weaknesses are. Useful — and to be held lightly. It's a starting hypothesis, not a finding.

## Rating literacy

Adults will hand you a number — usually a [DUPR](../vocabulary/term-dupr) rating. Be *literate* with it: fluent enough to talk in the language they use, to roughly group a clinic, to set expectations. But never *ruled* by it.

- A rating tells you *roughly where* a player sits. It tells you nothing about *why* they're stuck there.
- Two players at the same number can have completely different games and completely different needs.
- The rating is a starting hypothesis. Your eyes and [the Hierarchy of Assessment](../frameworks/hierarchy-of-assessment) do the real assessment.

## Assessment first, always

The hardest intake discipline: **the focus the player asks for is often not the focus they need.** A player says "fix my serve" — but the warm-up read shows the serve is fine and the real bleed is unforced errors off easy balls. Honor the request enough to keep their trust, but lead with the genuine diagnosis.

This is why every [1:1](structure-a-1-1-lesson) and [small group](run-a-small-group-lesson) opens with a real warm-up read. Intake gives you the hypothesis. The first session's warm-up tests it.

## Setting expectations

Close the intake by setting an honest expectation: roughly what the first session will look like, what kind of progress is realistic, how the running focus will work over time. An adult doing a cost-benefit calculation needs to know what they're buying. Honest, specific expectations — set up front — are a retention move, not a sales risk.

## Common failures

- **Skipping intake entirely** — showing up to lesson one knowing nothing, burning the first session on discovery you could have done in a five-minute call.
- **Taking the rating as gospel** — coaching the number instead of the player.
- **Coaching the requested focus blindly** — without testing whether it's the real one.
- **Overpromising** — setting an expectation of fast, dramatic progress you can't honor.

> Intake is a real conversation, not a formality. Get the why, hold the rating lightly, and remember the focus they asked for is a hypothesis your eyes still have to test.
