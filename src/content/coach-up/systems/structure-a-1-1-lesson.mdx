# How to Structure a 1:1 Lesson

*The private lesson is where every framework in this handbook gets its purest application — one player, your full attention, the [Hierarchy](../frameworks/hierarchy-of-assessment) running constantly. It's also your highest-rate work, and 100% yours.*

---

## The arc

A 1:1 compresses [the standard session arc](../frameworks/session-design-framework) into one focused player:

```
WARM & READ         Player warms up; you watch closely and take a
(first 5–8 min)     real diagnostic read. This is assessment time.

THE FRAME           Name today's focus and the why. With one player,
(1–2 min)           tie it directly to their running focus.

THE WORK            Constraint games and feeds against today's focus,
(the bulk)          sequenced easy to hard. The Hierarchy runs the
                    whole time — diagnose, then teach, one note.

LIVE PRESSURE       The focus has to survive game-speed play —
(~10 min)           live points, a game-like format, or you as a
                    live opponent.

CLOSE THE LOOP      One clear takeaway. Update their running focus.
(~3–5 min)          Name what next session builds toward.
```

## What the 1:1 demands

- **Real assessment first.** With a group you read on the fly; with a 1:1 you can actually *diagnose*. Spend the warm-up genuinely watching — walk [the Hierarchy](../frameworks/hierarchy-of-assessment) on what you see — before you commit to today's focus. Often the focus the player *asked* for isn't the focus they *need*; the warm-up read is how you find that out.
- **The running focus is everything.** A one-off 1:1 is fine; a *series* of 1:1s is where real development happens — and that only works if you track a [running focus](../vocabulary/term-running-focus) across sessions. Write it down. Each lesson is a chapter.
- **You still can't talk the whole time.** The trap of the 1:1 is over-coaching — one player, so the coach narrates everything. Resist it. The player still needs to *do* far more than they hear. Constraint games still beat monologue, even one-on-one.
- **Pace for one adult body.** One player gets a lot of reps fast and tires faster. Build in the micro-rests, watch for fatigue, and remember the older or newer body across from you ([Coaching Adults vs. Kids](../creed/coaching-adults-vs-kids)).

## Common failures

- **Skipping the read.** Jumping straight into the player's requested focus without diagnosing whether it's the right one.
- **Talking the lesson away.** A 1:1 is not a lecture with a paddle. Doing beats hearing here too.
- **No through-line.** Treating each lesson as standalone — no running focus, no memory, the player never compounds.
- **Forgetting it's a business relationship.** A 1:1 client is also a customer. The close — clear takeaway, sense of progress, a reason for next time — is retention. (See [Getting & Keeping Students](getting-and-keeping-students).)

## Where it fits

The 1:1 is your highest-rate, highest-feedback-density format, and under Coach Up's [rev-share](../vocabulary/term-rev-share) it's 100% your revenue. It's also where your diagnostic craft sharpens fastest — nothing else gives you this much uninterrupted reading and teaching of one player. Price it with a [rate card](../vocabulary/term-rate-card); intake it well ([Intake & Rating Literacy](intake-and-rating-literacy)).

> One player, full attention, the Hierarchy always running. Diagnose before the frame, track the running focus, and — even here — let them do far more than they hear.
