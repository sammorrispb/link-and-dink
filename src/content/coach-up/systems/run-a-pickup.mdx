# How to Run a Pickup

*The pickup is the apprentice's bread and butter — the event you'll run most, starting in week two. It looks casual. Running it* well *is not casual at all.*

---

## What a pickup is

An organized drop-in session: a set time, a set court, a rotation, and a coach who runs the room. Players pay a small fee; they come for a good two hours of matched, well-rotated games. The difference between a Coach Up pickup and "some people booked a court" is *you* — the rotation, the energy, the matching, the read.

## The arc

```
ARRIVE & WARM       Players warm themselves up. You greet everyone by
(first 10 min)      name, take a read on who's here and at what level.

SET THE ROTATION    You announce how it works — the format, the rotation,
(2 min)             the level mixing. Brief, confident, no ambiguity.

RUN THE ROOM        Games run. You manage the rotation, watch the
(the bulk)          matchups, adjust pairings, keep the energy up,
                    and coach lightly — a pickup is play-first.

CLOSE               Last round called clearly. Thank people by name.
(last 5 min)        Tell them the next one. Tell a friend.
```

## The decisions you own

- **The rotation.** Your core job. A good rotation keeps games competitive, keeps nobody sitting too long, and re-mixes the room so a wide level spread becomes variety instead of a problem ([The Mixed-Level Playbook](../frameworks/the-mixed-level-playbook)). Winners-split, partner-rotate, king-of-the-court — pick a rotation that fits the group size and *commit to it out loud*.
- **The matchups.** Reading the room never stops. A blowout game is a rotation you should have caught. Shuffle proactively, don't wait for someone to be miserable.
- **The energy.** A pickup with a flat coach is a flat pickup. You set the pace — [Active Court](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars) applies even when you're not "teaching."
- **The coaching dose.** A pickup is play-first. Coach *light* — a cue here, a reinforcement there, by name. Save real teaching for lessons and clinics; here, the coaching is seasoning, not the meal.

## Common failures

- **No announced system.** Players standing around asking "whose turn?" — that's a rotation you didn't set. Announce it, own it.
- **Coasting because "it's just a pickup."** It's a paid event with your name on it. Run it like one.
- **Over-coaching.** Stopping games to teach turns a fun pickup into a clinic nobody signed up for. Light touch.
- **Letting one player sit.** [Active Court](../frameworks/the-coach-up-pillars) — nobody benched by accident. If the numbers are awkward, the rotation absorbs it; you don't leave someone watching.

## Why it's the best rep-builder

The pickup throws everything at you at once — reading the room, managing a mixed group, energy, logistics, light coaching, the social close — in a low-stakes, play-first setting. It's the single best place to build the reflexes the rest of the craft depends on. Run a lot of them, and [debrief every one](../flows/post-event-debrief-flow).

> A pickup is play-first — but the rotation, the matching, the energy, and the read are all you. Casual for them, never for you.
