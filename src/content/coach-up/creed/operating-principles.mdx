# The Operating Principles

*The [Creed](the-coach-up-creed) is what we believe. These are the calls it makes for you when the session is moving and you don't have time to philosophize. Always, Never, and the warning signs.*

---

## Always

1. **Know every name and use it.** A player coached by name is a player who's seen. A player who's seen comes back.
2. **Give feedback out loud and in the moment.** Audible, not whispered. The whole court hearing "nice reset!" is the culture being built in real time.
3. **Reinforce before you correct.** Catch one good rep and name it before you fix anything. If you can't find a good rep, your task was set too hard — that's on you, not them.
4. **Diagnose before you teach.** Confirm intent and outcome before you touch technique. Walk [the Hierarchy](../frameworks/hierarchy-of-assessment), in order, every time.
5. **Keep bodies active.** Idle players don't learn and don't re-enroll. Shrink the lines, shrink the waits, design the breaks on purpose.
6. **Have a plan, and hold it loosely.** Walk in with a session designed. Then read the room and flex it. A plan you won't change is a script; no plan at all is improv. You want the third thing.
7. **End every session with one clear takeaway per player.** If a player can't say what they worked on, you were active but you didn't teach.
8. **Run your own debrief honestly.** Every session you coach gets a real post-mortem — what worked, the one thing you'd change, what you need before next time.
9. **Answer within 24 hours.** A question from a player, a parent, or your cohort gets a real reply inside a day. "I'm not sure, let me find out" counts.

## Never

1. **Never stop live play to deliver a correction.** Wait for the natural break — end of rally, end of point, water break. Stopping play to teach *feels* like teaching. It's the moment you lose the room.
2. **Never run a one-at-a-time line.** If three players are watching one player hit, you've built a drill that teaches one person and bores three. Redesign it.
3. **Never fix a stroke before you've confirmed the intent.** A technical fix to a shot the player wasn't trying to hit is wasted breath — and it tells the player you weren't really watching.
4. **Never let "good job" be the whole sentence.** Praise that isn't specific isn't feedback. Name the thing.
5. **Never coach the player you wish you had.** Coach the one in front of you — their level, their body, their reason for being there today.
6. **Never wing a session you could have planned.** Showing up empty and improvising off a basket of balls is the exact thing Coach Up exists to replace.
7. **Never make it weird.** Honest and specific, delivered warm. You can tell a player anything true if you've built the relationship to carry it.

## Warning signs — if you see this on a court, something is off

These are diagnostic, not disciplinary. If you spot one on your own court — or a cohort-mate's — it's a flag to act on, not a verdict.

- **The court is quiet.** Silence means disengagement. A working court has noise — rallies, callouts, the occasional cheer.
- **Players are standing in a line.** Lines are the universal tell of a session designed around the coach instead of the players.
- **The coach hasn't moved in five minutes.** A coach rooted to one spot has stopped reading the room.
- **Every piece of feedback is a correction.** No reinforcement means the coach is hunting for errors instead of building a player.
- **A player looks bored.** Boredom is a diagnostic — wrong level, wrong challenge, or wrong engagement. Read it and adjust; don't ignore it.
- **The constraint or game feels random.** If you can't say in one sentence what today's game is repping, the players definitely can't.
- **The coach is dodging the adult conversation.** Avoiding the player who needs a real conversation about level, goals, or fit — that catches up with you, every time.

## How to use this page

Don't memorize it. **Internalize the [Creed](the-coach-up-creed), and these fall out of it on their own.** This page is the checklist version — useful for your first cohorts, useful when you're tired, useful when you walk past a court and something feels off but you can't name it yet. The warning signs especially: train your eye on those, and you can self-correct a session before it goes sideways instead of after.

> A principle you have to look up isn't yours yet. Run the reps until it is.
