# Coaching Adults vs. Kids

*This handbook is seeded from the NGA Coaching System — a system built to coach kids 8–16. The pedagogy underneath it is sound and it carries over. But you are going to coach adults, and an adult is not a big kid. This page is the translation.*

---

## Why this page exists first

If you've ever been coached — in any sport, at any age — your instinct for "what coaching looks like" was probably formed in a youth setting. Drills, a whistle, a coach who decides everything. That instinct will quietly steer how you coach adults, and it will steer you wrong. So before the frameworks, the translation: here's what stays, and here's what has to change.

## What carries over, untouched

The **spine** of the system is age-independent, because it's built on how humans acquire motor skill, not on how children behave. All of this applies to a 9-year-old and a 59-year-old equally:

- **The environment teaches; you design it.** Constraint-led coaching works because of how perception and action are coupled — that's true at every age.
- **Diagnose before you teach.** [The Hierarchy of Assessment](../frameworks/hierarchy-of-assessment) — intent, outcome, technique, tactics — is a logic, not an age bracket.
- **External cues beat internal cues.** "Land it at their feet" outperforms "close your paddle face" for adults too.
- **Reinforce more than you correct.** Adults need the caught-good-rep just as much as kids do — arguably more, because they're more self-conscious about the bad ones.
- **The rally is the unit of learning.** True at every age.

If you only remember one thing: the *method* doesn't change. The *delivery* does.

## What has to change — the seven shifts

Adult learning has been studied for decades; the core findings are usually credited to Malcolm Knowles and the idea of **andragogy** (adult learning), as distinct from pedagogy (child learning). Pair that with the **Long-Term Athlete Development** model's recognition that most adult rec players are in the "Active for Life" stage — they're not chasing a podium, they're chasing enjoyment, fitness, and connection that lasts. (Both sources are in [Research Foundations](research-foundations).) Out of that, seven concrete shifts:

### 1. They need the *why* before the *how*

A kid will often run the drill because the coach said so. An adult won't — or will, resentfully, and won't retain it. Adults are problem-centered: they buy into a task once they understand what problem it solves for *them*. Lead with the why. "We're doing cross-court dinks only because the cross-court dink is the safest shot in the game and you're about to see why" — then run it.

### 2. They bring experience — an asset and an obstacle

A kid is often a blank page. An adult brings years of athletic habits, analogies from other sports, opinions, and ingrained movement patterns — some helpful, some load-bearing bad habits. Don't ignore the experience; **use it**. Connect the new skill to something they already own ("this is the same weight transfer as a tennis volley"). And respect that unlearning is harder and slower than learning — be patient with the ingrained stuff.

### 3. They are self-directed — coach *with* them, not *at* them

Adults have a self-concept of being responsible for their own lives, and they resist being managed. The youth-coach reflex — "do this, now this, now this" — reads as condescending to an adult. Offer the reasoning, offer some choice, and let them co-own the plan. This isn't softness; autonomy support measurably improves motor learning (see OPTIMAL theory in [Research Foundations](research-foundations)).

### 4. The ego bruises before the body does

Kids fail in front of each other all day and mostly don't care. Adults are acutely self-conscious about looking bad, especially in front of peers, especially if they're new. This is the single biggest retention risk you'll manage. Engineer early success. Make the first reps winnable. Normalize the miss out loud. Never let a struggling adult be the silent center of attention.

### 5. Time is scarce and protected

A kid's schedule is mostly decided for them. An adult chose to spend a protected, expensive slice of their week with you, instead of a hundred other things. That raises the bar: every session has to be visibly worth it. No wasted reps, no aimless drills, a clear takeaway they can feel. They are doing a cost-benefit calculation whether they say so or not.

### 6. They came partly for the people

For a lot of adult rec players, the social layer isn't a bonus — it's a primary reason they're there. "Active for Life" is as much about belonging as fitness. Design for it: pairings that mix the room, formats that let people talk, a court culture that's warm. A technically perfect session that left everyone isolated is a session that loses players.

### 7. The goal is lifelong play, not the next level

Most of your adults are not trying to become tournament players. They're trying to keep playing — well, happily, without injury — for years. Coach to that. Protect their bodies (warm-ups are not optional, load is real). Measure success in *do they still play, and do they still love it* — not in how fast you moved them up a rating. ([Getting and Keeping Students](../systems/getting-and-keeping-students) builds on this directly.)

## The translation table

| Dimension | Youth instinct | Coach Up's adult approach |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Buy-in | "Because I said so" | Lead with the why |
| Prior experience | Treat as blank page | Connect to what they already own |
| Authority | Coach decides everything | Co-own the plan; support autonomy |
| Failure | Mostly shrugged off | Engineer early success; protect the ego |
| Time | Decided for them | Scarce, protected — every rep must earn its place |
| Social layer | A nice extra | Often a primary reason they're there |
| Success metric | Move them up | Keep them playing — for years |

---

> Same method. Different human. Coach the adult in front of you — their reasons, their history, their body, their week — not the junior the drills were invented on.
