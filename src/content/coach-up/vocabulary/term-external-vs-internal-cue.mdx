# External vs. Internal Cue

*One of the most practical, immediately usable findings in motor-learning research — and a habit you can build today.*

**Definition.** An **external cue** directs a player's attention to the intended *effect* of a movement in the environment ("land it at their feet," "make it curve down into the kitchen"). An **internal cue** directs attention to the player's own *body mechanics* ("close your paddle face," "bend your knees"). Research consistently finds external cues produce faster learning and better performance under pressure.

**Why it matters.** It's [Creed belief #5](../creed/the-coach-up-creed) and a default mode for the whole handbook: coach where the ball goes, not how the arm moves. The same correction, reworded externally, simply works better.

**Coaching it (you, the coach).** Build the habit of *translating*. Every internal cue you're about to say has an external version — find it before you say it. Reach for an internal cue only when nothing external is landing, and get back to external fast. From Gabriele Wulf's attentional-focus research; see [OPTIMAL theory](term-optimal-theory) and [Research Foundations](../creed/research-foundations).
