# Repetition Without Repetition

*A phrase that sounds like a riddle and is actually a practice-design rule.*

**Definition.** The principle that effective practice repeats the *problem to be solved* rather than the *exact movement*. Each rep is slightly different — different ball, different angle, different pressure — so the player practices *solving*, not *grooving*. The phrase comes from the motor-control researcher Nikolai Bernstein.

**Why it matters.** A game never gives a player the identical ball twice. Practice that drills one identical rep over and over builds a narrow, brittle skill that breaks the moment the game varies it. Repeating the *problem* with varied reps builds an adaptable skill — one that holds up because variation was baked in from the start.

**Coaching it (you, the coach).** Concretely: vary your feed, vary the constraint, move the target. It's why [the Toolkit](../frameworks/the-constraint-design-toolkit) tells you to *turn one dial at a time* — you're constantly introducing controlled variation. It also connects to [differential learning](term-differential-learning) (Schöllhorn's stronger version of the same idea) and is part of why a [predictable feed is a coaching error](../frameworks/the-feedback-framework). See [Research Foundations](../creed/research-foundations).

> Repeat the problem, not the rep — vary it, because the game will.
