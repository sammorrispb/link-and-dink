# Engineered Success

*Designing the win. It feels like coddling. The research says it's mechanics.*

**Definition.** Deliberately designing a practice task — through the target size, the feed, the constraint, the difficulty — so that the player *succeeds* early and often, especially at the start of a session or a new skill. In the research it appears as *enhanced expectancies*: a learner who expects to succeed actually learns the movement faster.

**Why it matters.** A new coach's instinct is to make practice "challenging" from the first rep, treating early success as something the player should *earn*. The research (see [OPTIMAL theory](term-optimal-theory)) flips that: success is a *learning input*, not a reward. And for adults specifically — self-conscious, ego-protective — an engineered early win is also the single best retention move you have.

**Coaching it (you, the coach).** It's baked into [the Session Design Framework](../frameworks/session-design-framework): sequence easy to hard, start where the player can win. If a player can't find a rep to reinforce, the task was set too hard — that's your design failure to fix, not their flaw to expose. Then you *climb* — engineered success is the floor you build up from, not the whole session.

> Build the first win on purpose — confidence is a learning input, not a reward.
