# Representative Design

*The quality test for any drill or game you design: does it represent the real thing?*

**Definition.** The principle that a practice task should faithfully *represent* the conditions of real play — the same kinds of balls, reads, decisions, and pressure the player will actually face — so that what's learned in practice transfers to the game.

**Why it matters.** It explains *why* a perfectly executed drill can fail to make someone a better player. A player who can hit fifty perfect dinks off a coach's gentle, predictable feed has learned to handle a gentle, predictable feed — which doesn't exist in a game. Representative design is the standard that keeps practice honest.

**Coaching it (you, the coach).** Use it as a filter on your [session design](../frameworks/session-design-framework). For every drill or game, ask: *what's missing here that the real game has?* — and add it back through a [constraint](term-constraint-led-coaching). A fed-ball block can be a fine starting point, but the session should *climb back toward representative* before it ends, which is exactly why [the session arc](../frameworks/session-design-framework) ends in live application. From the ecological-dynamics research tradition — see [Research Foundations](../creed/research-foundations).

> If it doesn't look like the game, it won't survive the game.
