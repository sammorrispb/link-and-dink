# Whole-Part-Whole

*The structured exception to "always teach with a game." When you do need to isolate a piece, this is how to do it without losing the game.*

**Definition.** A teaching sequence: first the player attempts the *whole* skill in context; then the coach *isolates* the specific part that's breaking down for focused work; then the part is immediately returned to the *whole* so it re-integrates with everything else.

**Why it matters.** [Game-based teaching](term-game-based-teaching) is the default, but sometimes a game alone won't fix a genuinely broken part — the player needs a few isolated reps to find a movement. Whole-part-whole is how you do that isolation *responsibly*: it's bookended by the whole, so the part never becomes an orphaned drill that doesn't transfer.

**Coaching it (you, the coach).** The discipline is the final "whole." A new coach isolates a part and then forgets to put it back — and the player ends up with a clean drilled motion that collapses in a real rally ([representative design](term-representative-design) failure). The isolation is a brief detour, not a destination. Think of the "part" as a temporary [off-ramp](../frameworks/the-constraint-design-toolkit) — used, then exited.

> Whole first, isolate only what's broken, then put it right back in the game.
